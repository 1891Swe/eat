Understanding Your Results:

  • BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate): The number of calories your body needs to perform basic, life-sustaining functions.
  • TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure): Your BMR plus additional calories burned through daily activities and exercise.
  • Daily Calorie Target: Adjusted calories based on your weight management goal.

Note: These calculations provide estimates based on population averages. Individual metabolism may vary. Always consult with a healthcare professional before making significant changes to your diet.

Heart Rate Zone Training:

  • Zone 1 (50-60%): Very light activity, warm up, recovery, improves general health.
  • Zone 2 (60-70%): Light activity, improves basic endurance and fat burning.
  • Zone 3 (70-80%): Moderate activity, improves aerobic fitness and efficiency.
  • Zone 4 (80-90%): Hard activity, improves anaerobic fitness and performance.
  • Zone 5 (90-100%): Maximum effort, improves maximum performance and speed.

Note: Heart rate zones are estimates. Individual factors like fitness level, medications, and environmental conditions can affect your actual heart rate response.

Common Fitness Conversions

  • 1 pound of body weight = 3,500 calories (approx)
  • 1 MET (Metabolic Equivalent) = 3.5 ml oxygen/kg/min
  • 1 mile running/walking ≈ 100 calories (varies by weight)

Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate are these fitness calculators?

All calculators provide estimates based on established formulas and population averages. Individual results may vary due to factors like body composition, genetics, and activity level. For the most accurate assessment, consult with healthcare professionals.

What is the difference between BMR and TDEE?

BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate) is the number of calories your body needs at complete rest to maintain basic functions. TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure) includes BMR plus additional calories burned through daily activities and exercise.

Why might the Navy body fat formula be inaccurate for some people?

The Navy formula relies on circumference measurements and doesn't directly measure body fat. It may be less accurate for people with atypical body shapes, very muscular builds, or those at the extremes of height and weight ranges.

How often should I train in different heart rate zones?

For balanced training, most fitness experts recommend spending the majority (about 80%) of your workout time in Zones 1-2 and the remainder (about 20%) in Zones 3-5. However, the optimal distribution depends on your fitness goals, sport, and training phase.
